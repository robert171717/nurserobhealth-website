🧵 The FDA just announced their July compounding committee meeting. Here's what it actually means for peptides — not what influencers are telling you.

---

**Reply 1:**
1/ As a licensed RN, I've been tracking the FDA's moves on peptide compounding closely. The Pharmacy Compounding Advisory Committee meets July 23-24 to decide which peptides make the 503A Bulks List. Let me break this down the RIGHT way.

**Reply 2:**
2/ First, what's the 503A Bulks List? It's the FDA's official list of bulk drug substances that compounding pharmacies CAN legally use. If a peptide isn't on it, compounding it gets... complicated.

**Reply 3:**
3/ The FDA is considering 14 peptides for this list. Some familiar names: BPC-157, TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4), AOD-9604, MOTS-c, and others. This is a BIG moment for the peptide space.

**Reply 4:**
4/ Here's what the research actually shows: BPC-157 has compelling animal data on soft tissue healing, but human trials? Almost none. TB-500 has wound-healing promise but the evidence base is thin. We need more data — I'm watching closely.

**Reply 5:**
5/ The myth: "These peptides are about to be banned." Reality: The FDA is evaluating whether to INCLUDE them on the compounding list. This could actually IMPROVE access through regulated channels. Nuance matters.

**Reply 6:**
6/ Safety: As a nurse, what I check for — contamination risk from unregulated sources, allergic reactions, and drug interactions. A peptide from a licensed compounding pharmacy under FDA oversight is fundamentally different from a vial off the internet.

**Reply 7:**
7/ Bottom line: July 2026 could reshape peptide access in America. Pay attention, but don't panic.

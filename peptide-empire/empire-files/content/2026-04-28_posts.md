🧵 THREAD: The FDA is reviewing 12 peptides RIGHT NOW — here's what every biohacker needs to know

2/ First — quick history. In late 2023, the FDA moved 19 peptides from Category 1 (allowed for compounding) to Category 2 (not allowed). This effectively removed BPC-157, TB-500, MOTS-C, and others from pharmacy access overnight. No clinical trial. No safety review. Just a reclassification.

3/ What changed? In February 2026, HHS Secretary RFK Jr. went on Joe Rogan's podcast and said the FDA "illegally reclassified" those peptides during the Biden administration. • BPC-157
• TB-500
• KPV
• MOTS-C
• Semax
• Epitalon

6/ IMPORTANT CAVEAT: Reclassification ≠ FDA approval. If a peptide moves back to Category 1, that means compounding pharmacies CAN produce it — but it still hasn't gone through randomized controlled trials for safety and efficacy. A compounded peptide is NOT an FDA-approved drug. I can't say this loudly enough.

7/ Bottom line: The peptide access landscape could look very different by fall 2026. But "available" and "proven" are two different things. As a nurse, my advice doesn't change: research the compound, know your source, talk to your doctor, and understand the data gaps.

## Post 2: Myth-Busting Engagement — "The FDA Only Banned Peptides Because They're Dangerous"
**Time:** 12:00 PM MST | **Format:** Hot Take / Myth-Bust | **CTA:** Comment + Save

🔥 HOT TAKE: "The FDA banned peptides because they're dangerous."

This is the #1 myth I see in biohacking communities. Let me correct it as a licensed RN who actually reads the regulatory filings.

Here's what ACTUALLY happened in 2023:

The FDA moved 19 peptides to Category 2 — but NOT because they were proven dangerous. It was an administrative reclassification based on a lack of sufficient safety documentation submitted to the agency. That's a paperwork gap, not a toxicity finding.

Clinical reality: Many of these peptides (BPC-157, TB-500, GHK-Cu) have been used in research settings for years with well-characterized safety profiles. BPC-157 in particular has been studied since the 1990s with no reported serious adverse events in published literature.

The real risk from the 2023 ban? It pushed demand into the black market. RFK Jr. himself pointed this out: the ban created "very, very substandard" underground products with no quality control, no sterility testing, and no accountability.

So no — the FDA didn't ban these because they're dangerous. They banned them because the paperwork wasn't there. And now they're reconsidering.

The lesson: When you hear "the FDA banned it," ask WHY. There's usually more to the story.

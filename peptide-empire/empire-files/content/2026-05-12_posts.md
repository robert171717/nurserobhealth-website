```
🧵 As a licensed RN, here's what the research actually says about peptide routes of administration — and why most online advice gets it wrong.

1/ I'll keep it simple: injection, oral, nasal spray, topical — they're not interchangeable. The route changes how much of a peptide reaches your cells. Most people pick a route before understanding the pharmacokinetics. That's backwards.

2/ Here's the problem: most peptides have short half-lives. BPC-157 clears plasma in under 30 minutes. A 2025 systematic review in HSS Journal® (Vasireddi et al.) found only 1 human study out of 36 reviewed — and that was intra-articular, not subQ or oral.

3/ Oral peptides? Stomach acid degrades most before they reach circulation. SubQ bypasses digestion but absorption varies by injection site. IV gives 100% bioavailability but requires medical supervision. Each route is effectively a different delivery system.

4/ A 2025 University of Utah review in Current Reviews in Musculoskeletal Medicine called BPC-157 "investigational" — and that's the most researched peptide in this space. If we lack solid human data for BPC-157, we have even less for MOTS-C, TB-500, or GHK-Cu.

5/ What this means practically: when someone claims one route is superior without citing comparative human data, they're guessing. The research gap isn't a secret — it's just rarely acknowledged. Transparency about what we don't know is clinical responsibility.

6/ Bottom line: route determines bioavailability — and we lack head-to-head human trials. Choose routes based on published protocols, not influencer claims.
```

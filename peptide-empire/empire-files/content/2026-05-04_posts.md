🧵 Your mitochondria aren't broken. They're just tired. Here's what NAD+ actually does — and why the research is more nuanced than the marketing.

--- REPLY 1 ---
1/ As a licensed RN, I've watched NAD+ go from niche biochemistry to mainstream supplement aisle in about 3 years. Let me break down what we actually know — and what we're still guessing on.

--- REPLY 2 ---
2/ First, what is NAD+? Nicotinamide Adenine Dinucleotide. It's a coenzyme found in every living cell. Without it, your mitochondria can't convert food into energy. Think of it as the spark plug in your cellular engine.

--- REPLY 3 ---
3/ Research shows NAD+ drops ~50% between age 40 and 60 — correlated with metabolic dysfunction, cognitive decline, and reduced DNA repair. The real question isn't whether NAD+ matters. It's whether supplementing reverses any of this.

--- REPLY 4 ---
4/ Dosing: Research protocols typically use 300-1000mg IV NAD+ or 250-500mg subcutaneous NAD+ precursors (NMN/NR). Oral bioavailability is very limited — most studies use injection routes. This is NOT medical advice — these are research observations.

--- REPLY 5 ---
5/ Stacking: NAD+ pairs well with TMG (trimethylglycine) to support methylation, and resveratrol for sirtuin activation. What I'd avoid: stacking with high-dose niacin (flush overlap) or taking late in the day (can disrupt sleep cycle).

--- REPLY 6 ---
6/ Safety: As a nurse, here's what I check — kidney function (NAD+ is renally cleared), methylation status, and if you're on BP meds. Flushing and nausea are real but usually transient. Long-term safety data? Still limited — we're at 2-3 year follow-ups max.

--- REPLY 7 ---
7/ Bottom line: NAD+ isn't the fountain of youth — but it IS one of the most promising longevity areas. Wide gap between proven and marketed. Stay skeptical, stay curious.
